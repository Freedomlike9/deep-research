# Counter Evidence

Use this skill to make the research process adversarial in a healthy way.

## When To Use

Use this skill when:

- a topic could be presented too positively or too negatively
- the report may include recommendations or judgments
- a finding seems important but is supported by limited evidence
- the topic includes technology choices, market comparisons, or claims about superiority

Examples:

- "Is framework A better than framework B"
- "Should we adopt protocol X"
- "Why is this project successful"
- "What are the long-term risks of this architecture"

## Goal

Prevent one-sided reports by forcing the research process to look for disconfirming evidence.

## Workflow

1. Identify the highest-impact claims in the working findings.
2. For each claim, ask what would make the claim weaker, conditional, or false.
3. Search for limitations, alternatives, criticism, tradeoffs, failure cases, and implementation pain points.
4. Preserve strong counterarguments even if the main conclusion still stands.
5. If no counter-evidence is found, say that the search did not surface strong opposing evidence rather than claiming none exists.

## Counter-Query Patterns

Useful patterns include:

- "{topic} limitations"
- "{topic} criticism"
- "{topic} risks"
- "{topic} alternatives"
- "{topic} failure case"
- "{topic} issues"
- "{topic} controversy"
- "{topic} vs alternative"

## Output Expectations

When this skill is active:

- include at least one section or paragraph on risks, objections, or limits
- downgrade confidence when counter-evidence is unresolved
- distinguish between "no strong counter-evidence found" and "claim is proven"
- include mixed judgments when the evidence is genuinely split

## Decision Rule

A conclusion is stronger after this skill only if it survives serious attempts to challenge it.
