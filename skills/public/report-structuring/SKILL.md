# Report Structuring

Use this skill to turn raw notes and findings into a readable research deliverable.

## When To Use

Use this skill when:

- the final output should be shared with others
- the topic is broad enough to require hierarchy and synthesis
- raw findings are useful but still feel fragmented
- the report needs to support decision-making, not just information collection

## Writing Goals

The report should be:

- easy to scan
- explicit about scope and method
- strong on key findings
- honest about risks and information gaps
- written as a report, not as a dump of search notes

## Recommended Structure

A strong general-purpose research report usually includes:

1. Title
2. One-sentence thesis or executive takeaway
3. Executive summary
4. Scope and method
5. Key findings
6. Cases, examples, or data points
7. Risks and limitations
8. Trend or forward-looking judgment
9. Source list

## Section Guidance

### Executive summary
- summarize the most decision-relevant findings
- keep it short and concrete

### Scope and method
- define what was researched
- clarify the evidence boundary and approach

### Key findings
- group related conclusions
- present claims first, then supporting evidence
- use lists or tables when comparison helps

### Risks and limitations
- always include uncertainty, tradeoffs, or boundary conditions
- do not hide weak evidence

### Trend or forward-looking judgment
- distinguish projection from verified fact
- keep judgment tied to evidence, not rhetoric

## Formatting Guidance

- prefer short paragraphs and clear headings
- use bullets for comparisons, pros and cons, and evidence summaries
- use tables when multiple alternatives or metrics are compared
- keep the source list clean and easy to map back to the main text

## Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- mixing raw notes with final prose
- burying the main conclusion deep in the report
- omitting the limits of the evidence
- presenting speculation in the same tone as verified facts
